### How to Choose a Dog Bowl

1. **Material matters** — Stainless steel is the safest (non-porous, dishwasher-safe, won't chip). Ceramic is great but inspect for chips. Plastic can cause chin acne and harbors bacteria — avoid if possible.
2. **Size match** — Bowls should be proportional to your dog. A 70lb Lab needs 4-8 cups. A 10lb Chihuahua needs 1 cup. Too-large bowls encourage gulping. Too-small bowls frustrate the dog.
3. **Elevated vs floor feeding** — Elevated bowls (chest height) are better for large breeds (reduces bloat risk) and senior dogs (easier on joints). Floor feeding is fine for small/medium dogs with good posture.
4. **Non-skid base** — Essential if your dog pushes bowls around. Rubber or silicone bottoms work best on smooth floors. Weighted ceramic bowls are naturally stable.
5. **Cleanability** — Dishwasher-safe is a must. Removable inserts are ideal (easier to clean than fixed bowls). Avoid bowls with crevices where food can hide.

## FAQ

**Are plastic dog bowls bad for dogs?**

They can be—plastic bowls develop scratches where bacteria hide and can cause chin acne (feline acne in cats too). Some dogs develop plastic allergies. Stainless steel or ceramic is always safer.

**How often should I wash my dog's bowl?**

Daily for food bowls (bacteria grows in leftover food). Every 2-3 days for water bowls (slime/biofilm forms). Use hot water and soap or run through the dishwasher. A clean bowl is basic hygiene.

**Do elevated dog bowls prevent bloat?**

Research is mixed. Some studies suggest elevated bowls may INCREASE bloat risk in large, deep-chested breeds. Others say it helps digestion. Consult your vet — for most dogs, floor-level feeding is fine.

**What size bowl does my dog need?**

Food bowl: should hold 1.5x your dog's meal portion. Water bowl: 2-4 cups minimum for medium dogs, 6-8 cups for large breeds. Bigger is better for water (freshness lasts longer).
